Tim Berners-Lee

The man who invented the World Wide Web

Here's a timeline of Sir Tim Berners-Lee's life:

  • 1955 - Born in London, England.
  • 1976 - Receives a first-class bachelor of arts degree in physics.
  • 1978 - Joins D. G. Nash in Ferndown, Dorset, where he helped create type-setting software for printers.
  • 1980 - Works as an independent contractor at CERN for some months. Leaves CERN and goes to work at John Poole's Image Computer Systems, Ltd, in Bournemouth, Dorset, where he runs the company's technical side for three years. The project he works on is a "real-time remote procedure call" which gives him experience in computer networking.
  • 1984 - Returns to CERN as a fellow.
  • 1989 - Proposes an information management system and then implements the first successful communication between a Hypertext Transfer Protocol (HTTP) client and server via the Internet.
  • 1990 - Redistributes his proposal and is accepted by his manager. Marries Nancy Carlson, an American computer programmer.
    Publishes the first web site.
  • 1994 - Founds the W3C at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology.
  • 1995 - Receives ACM Software System Award.
  • 2001 - Becomes a patron of the East Dorset Heritage Trust, having previously lived in Colehill in Wimborne, East Dorset.
    Is elected a Fellow of the Royal Society (FRS).
  • 2004 - Is knighted by Queen Elizabeth II for his pioneering work.
    Accepts a chair in computer science at the School of Electronics and Computer Science, University of Southampton, Hampshire, to work on the Semantic Web.
  • 2007 - Is appointed to the Order of Merit (OM), an order restricted to 24 (living) members.
  • 2009 - Is elected Foreign Associate of the National Academy of Sciences. Launches the World Wide Web Foundation (WWWF) in order to campaign to "advance the Web to empower humanity by launching transformative programs that build local capacity to leverage the Web as a medium for positive change".
  • 2011 - Divorces Nancy Carlson​.
  • 2012 - Co-founds with Nigel Shadbolt the Open Data Institute.
    Is among the British cultural icons selected by artist Sir Peter Blake to appear in a new version of his most famous artwork – the Beatles' Sgt. Pepper's Lonely Hearts Club Band album cover – to celebrate the British cultural figures of his life that he most admires to mark his 80th birthday.
  • 2013 - Is awarded the inaugural Queen Elizabeth Prize for Engineering.
  • 2014 - Marries Rosemary Leith, Canadian Internet and banking entrepreneur and a founding director of Berners-Lee's World Wide Web Foundation.
  • 2016 - Receives Turing Award "for inventing the World Wide Web, the first web browser, and the fundamental protocols and algorithms allowing the Web to scale".
    Joins the Department of Computer Science at Oxford University as a professorial research fellow and as a fellow of Christ Church, one of the Oxford colleges.
  • 2018 - Announces his new open-source startup Inrupt to fuel a commercial ecosystem around the Solid project, which aims to give users more control over their personal data and lets users choose where the data goes, who's allowed to see certain elements and which apps are allowed to see that data.
  • 2019 - Launches Contract for the Web, a campaign initiative to persuade governments, companies and citizens to commit to nine principles to stop "misuse" with the warning that "if we don't act now – and act together – to prevent the web being misused by those who want to exploit, divide and undermine, we are at risk of squandering [its potential for good]".

"He wove the World Wide Web and created a mass medium for the 21st century. The World Wide Web is Berners-Lee's alone. He designed it. He loosed it on the world. And he more than anyone else has fought to keep it open, nonproprietary and free."

—Tim Berners-Lee's entry in Time magazine's list of the 100 Most Important People of the 20th century, March 1999
